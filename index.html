INTRODUCTION

INTRODUCTION

  • crucial interrelation between microstructure and chemical, physical, and/or mechanical properties of materials
  • In this course: characterization of the microstructure both qualitative and quantitative, as well as the chemical composition.

INTRODUCTION

  • The microstructural and chemical characterization relies mostly on the interaction of a material with a probe.
    • visible light
    • X-rays
    • beam of high energy electrons
  • The resultant signal will then be collected, processed and interpreted.

INTRODUCTION

  • Microscopy: allows looking at objects not visible for the naked eye.
  • Limitation: the smallest distinguishable features are determined by the wavelength of visual light (0.4 - 0.7 μm)
  • => smaller wavelength => smaller details can be observed

Overview of various types of EM-radiation and their wavelength

  • electrons can be accelerated up to a velocity near the speed of light => electrons with a wavelength ≈ 0.1 nm => atomic and molecular scale of matter.
  • electron microscopes: electrons emitted from a heated filament are accelerated by a high voltage and then focused using magnetic lenses onto a sample; resolution in these devices is limited by lenses.
  • X-rays typically have a wavelength below 10 nm => in practice resolution is limited because of other reasons (focusing devices).
  • Their penetrating power is largely dependent on their energy:
    • The energy of an X-ray is reversely proportional to its wavelength and is calculated by the equation E=hc/lambda
  • X-rays are not only for microscopic purposes (µm size), but in general provides a method for non-destructive imaging of objects of all sizes (up to meters).
  • fast development in characterization techniques
  • digital revolution

=> massive amounts of digital data

  • up to the geologist to make a critical decision associated with
    • sample selection
    • sample preparation
    • choice of characterization method
    • understanding and critical review of results
  • In this course we hope to clarify these steps, in order to prepare the geologist for a scientific way of interpreting digital data obtained by the techniques.

Some general terms

  • MACROSTRUCTURE: Those features visible to the naked eye.
    • large pores
    • big inclusions
    • cracks

  • MACROSTRUCTURE: Those features visible to the naked eye.
  • MESOSTRUCTURE: those features that are on the borderline of the visible.
  • MICROSTRUCTURE: covers the scale of structural phenomena most commonly of concern to geologists, namely grain and particle size, microcracking and microporosity.
  • NANOSTRUCTURE: restricted to sub-micrometre features including the width of grain boundaries. A nanostructure is an object of intermediate size between microscopic and molecular structures.

Applications in Geology

  • link between nano-, micro and macroscale processes inside porous material

What are "DIGITAL IMAGES"?

DIGITAL IMAGES

  • electronic snapshots taken of a scene or scanned from documents
  • sampled and mapped as a grid of dots or picture elements (pixels).

can anyone explain what a pixel is?

A PIXEL

In digital imaging, a pixel, or picture element is a physical point in a raster image, or the smallest addressable element in an all points addressable display device; so it is the smallest controllable element of a picture represented on the screen.

  • Each pixel is assigned a tonal value (black, white, shades of gray or colour), which is represented in binary code.
  • The binary digits ("bits") for each pixel are stored in a sequence by a computer and often reduced to a mathematical representation (compressed). The bits are then interpreted and read by the computer to produce an analog version for display or printing.

DIGITAL IMAGES

RESOLUTION

  • = the ability to distinguish fine spatial detail.
  • The spatial frequency at which a digital image is sampled (the sampling frequency) is often a good indicator of resolution.
  • Dots-per-inch (dpi) or pixels-per-inch (ppi) are common and synonymous terms used to express resolution for digital images.

As the spatial frequency drops (features become larger), the required contrast increases

RESOLUTION

  • Pixels: Individual pixels can be seen by zooming in an image.
  • the more pixels an image contains, the more it can be enlarged before the separate pixels start to show

RESOLUTION

  • Very important parameter for the selection of the camera => Images must be able to record the fine detail revealed by the microscope magnification.
  • often quoted as the size of charged coupled device (CCD), which is effectively the number of pixels on the chip. It should be noted: the size of the individual pixels varies amongst different types of CCDs.
  • Increasing resolution enables the capture of finer detail, but at some point added resolution will not result in an appreciable gain in image quality, only larger file size.

Effects of resolution on image quality

PIXEL DIMENSIONS

  • = the horizontal and vertical measurements of an image expressed in pixels.
  • The pixel dimensions may be determined by multiplying the width and the height by the dpi.

PIXEL DIMENSIONS

  • A digital camera also has pixel dimensions, expressed as the number of pixels horizontally and vertically that define its resolution
  • Example: An 8" x 10" document that is scanned at 300 dpi has the pixel dimensions of 2,400 pixels (8" x 300 dpi) by 3,000 pixels (10" x 300 dpi).

Can anyone tell what "bit depth" means?

BIT DEPTH

  • = determined by the number of bits used to define each pixel.
  • The greater the bit depth, the greater the number of tones (grayscale or color) that can be represented
  • Digital images
    • black and white (bitonal)
    • greyscale
    • colour

BITH DEPTH

  • Bitonal image
    • bit depth = 1
    • pixels can represent two tones
      • typically black and white

BITH DEPTH

  • Greyscale image
    • bit depth > 1
      • typically from 2 to 8 bits or more.

BIT DEPTH

  • example: a 2-bit image
    • 4 possible combinations: 00, 01, 10 and 11.
    • If 00 = black and 11 = white: then 01 = dark grey and 10 = light grey.
    • bit depth = 2
    • number of tones that can be represented is 2(bit depth) or 4.
    • at 8 bits:256 = 28

    • tipically a bith depth from 8 to 24 or higher
    • 24-bit image: bits are often divided in 3 groupings (RGB) combination of those bits are used to represent the whole colour spectrum
    • 24-bit image: 16.7 million (224) clour values

DYNAMIC RANGE

  • = the range of tonal difference between the lightest light and darkest dark of an image.
  • a higher dynamic range
    • = more tones can be represented
    • /= number of tones actually reproduced
  • Dynamic range also describes a digital system's ability to reproduce tonal information.
